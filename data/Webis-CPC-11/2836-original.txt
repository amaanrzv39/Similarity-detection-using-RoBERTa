He thought for himself, and yet he could
assimilate the ideas of other men. He was a reasoner and a doctrinaire; and yet he must have
had in himself those untamed volcanic emotions which we associate with the heroes of the romantic
novels of the age. He believed in the almost unlimited powers of the human mind, and his own
career, which saw his rise from stable-boy and cobbler to dramatist, was itself a monument
to the human will. Looking in their mirrors, the progressives of that generation were tempted
to think that perfection might have been within their reach had not their youth been stunted
by the influence of Calvin and the British Constitution. Rectitude, courage and unflinching
truth were Holcroft's ideal.
