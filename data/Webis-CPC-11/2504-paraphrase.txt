Is not diplomacy, nastily called by Voltaire the paddock of lies, as competent as ever it was to dupe administrators and administered by noble abstract catchwords veiling obscure and perplexing intents, and spinning the total world over with life-force and tears, to a bizarre Witch's Sabbath?"[11] This is his resolution of the total matter.

[Footnote 11: Recollections: II, p. 366 ff.]

But while the justifications for the nonachievement are not far to look for, it is worth while for the preacher to dwell on them for a moment. In toughly middled characters like a Morley or an Erasmus, humanism creates a stoical endurance and a sublime self-confidence. But it tends, in lesser spirits, to a impatient arrogance.
