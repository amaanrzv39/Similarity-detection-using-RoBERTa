We remove the substantial object. The evident object, too, disappears: it departs its place. We restore the substantial object--the evident object reappears in statu quo. There is no event to alter the experiment. If we find that the evident object always departs its location when the substantial object departs its, and that the one always arrives back when the other comes back, we have conveyed ahead rather sufficient to set up an inescapable association in location between the two. The two locations are from now on considered, not as two, but as one and the same. 
By the help of the feel, then, we have affiliated the visual phenomena with thing which are not the body part of vision; and well it is for us that we have finished so betimes, and before we were cognizant of the eye's existence.
