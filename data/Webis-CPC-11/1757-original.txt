As a matter of fact, in his introduction to the Keys, which are
entitled Lemuel Gulliver's Travels into Several Remote Nations of the World. Compendiously
Methodized, For Publick Benefit: With Observations and Explanatory Notes Throughout (1726),
Curll flatters Swift as possessing "the true Vein of Humour and polite Conversation" (I, 4).
Regarding the Travels, he observes, "The Town are infinitely more eager after them than they
were after Robinson Crusoe" (I, 5).
